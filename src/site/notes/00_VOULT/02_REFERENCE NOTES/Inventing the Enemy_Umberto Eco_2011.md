They have no external enemies, and in any case they are never able to agree on who those enemies are, because they are constantly at war with one another: Pisa against Lucca, Guelfs against Ghibellines, Northerners against Southerners, Fascists against Partisans, the Mafia against the State, the government against the judiciary. 13/10/25 11:47

Having an enemy is important not only for defining our identity but also for providing us with an obstacle against which to measure our system of values and to demonstrate, in confronting it, our own worth. 13/10/25 11:48

A new form of enemy would then emerge, with the development of contacts between peoples: not only the one who stays outside and displays his strangeness from afar, but the one who is inside, among us — what we would today call the non-EU immigrant — who in some way behaves differently or speaks our language poorly. 13/10/25 11:59

In the complexion of negroes we encounter various shades; but they all alike differ from other men in the features of their faces. Round cheeks, high cheekbones, a slightly elevated forehead, a short, broad and flat nose, thick lips, small ears, ugliness and irregularity of shape characterize their external appearance. For the male who governs and writes, or governs by writing, woman has been depicted as an enemy from the very beginning. 13/10/25 12:11

From what we might call “normal” misogyny we arrive at the construction of the witch, the masterpiece of modern civilization. Certainly the witch was known also in classical antiquity, and I will limit myself to recalling Horace (“I myself saw Canidia, dressed in a black robe, with bare feet and disheveled hair, howling together with the elder Sagana”). 13/10/25 12:13

---

Instead, it is at the dawn of the modern world that the witch begins to gather in covens, to celebrate her sabbaths, to fly, to transform herself into an animal, and to become a social enemy so much so as to deserve inquisitorial trials and the stake. 13/10/25 12:14

---

In witchcraft trials, not only is an image of the enemy constructed, and not only does the victim eventually confess even what she has not done, but in confessing it she convinces herself that she has actually done it. 13/10/25 12:20

---

The same thing happened in the Stalinist trials: first the image of the enemy was constructed, and then the victim was persuaded to recognize herself in that image.

The construction of the enemy leads even those who would have aspired to benevolent recognition to become that enemy. Theater and narrative show us examples of the “ugly duckling” who, despised by their peers, end up conforming to the image others have of them. A typical example I would cite is Richard III. 13/10/25 12:20

---

It seems we cannot do without the enemy. The figure of the enemy cannot be abolished by the processes of civilization. This need is inherent even in the gentle man who loves peace. In such cases the image of the enemy is simply shifted from a human object to a natural or social force that in some way threatens us and must be overcome — whether it be capitalist exploitation, environmental pollution, or the hunger of the Third World. 13/10/25 12:21

---

Is ethics therefore powerless in the face of this ancestral need to have enemies? I would say that the ethical demand arises not when we pretend that there are no enemies, but when we try to understand them, to put ourselves in their shoes. There is no hostility toward the Persians in Aeschylus; he experiences their tragedy among them and from their point of view. Caesar treats the Gauls with great respect, at most portraying them as a bit whiny whenever they surrender, and Tacitus admires the Germans, finding them even of fine physique, limiting himself to lamenting their filth and their reluctance to do hard work because they cannot stand heat and thirst. 13/10/25 12:22

---

These forms of understanding the enemy belong to poets, saints, or traitors. Our deepest impulses are of a quite different order. In 1968 a secret report was published in America titled Report from Iron Mountain on the Possibility and Desirability of Peace, by Anonymous (and some even attributed it to Galbraith). Clearly it was a pamphlet against war, or at least a pessimistic lament about its inevitability. But since making war requires an enemy to fight against, the inevitability of war corresponds to the inevitability of identifying and constructing the enemy. 13/10/25 12:23

---

…because only war constitutes the foundation of the harmonious development of human societies. Its organized waste constitutes a valve that regulates the smooth functioning of society. It solves the problem of surpluses; it is a flywheel. War allows a community to recognize itself as a “nation”; without the counterpart of war a government could not even establish the sphere of its own legitimacy; only war ensures the balance between classes and allows antisocial elements to be placed and exploited. Peace produces instability and juvenile delinquency; war channels all turbulent forces in the most appropriate way, giving them a “status.” The army is the last hope of the dispossessed and the maladjusted; only the war system, with its power of life and death, disposes society to pay a blood price even for other institutions that do not depend on it, such as the development of the automobile industry. 13/10/25 12:24

---

It is war that allows the development of a truly “humanistic” art, in which situations of conflict predominate.

If this is so, the construction of the enemy must be intensive and constant. George Orwell offers us a truly exemplary model of this in 1984: 13/10/25 12:24

The horrible thing about the Two Minutes Hate was not that one had to take part in it, but, on the contrary, that it was impossible to avoid joining in the chorus of execration.
