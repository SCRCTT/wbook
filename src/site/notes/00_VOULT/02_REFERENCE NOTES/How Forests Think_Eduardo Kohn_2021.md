**How Forests Think – Eduardo Kohn**

**Introduction** Studying the human through the non-human—that is, studying the signs and representations of animals (etc.) to study the human.

“All beings, including non-human ones, are constitutively semiotic. All life is semiotic and all semiosis is alive. In decisive ways, then, life and thought are the exact same thing: life thinks; thoughts are alive.”

“Ghassan Hage (2012) defines ‘alter-politics’—a politics that does not develop from opposition to our current systems or from their critique, but develops from the analysis of another way of being, which here involves other living beings.”

Just as when one studies and compares the ant system.

“Being aware of the selfhood of the multiple beings that populate the cosmos poses particular challenges. The Runa enter the forest’s ecology of selves to hunt, which means they recognize others as selves like themselves in order to transform them into non-selves.”

Death, becoming objects.

“Much of this chapter focuses on the semiotic analysis of human attempts to understand and be understood by their dogs. For example, the people of Ávila attempt to interpret their dogs' dreams, even administering psychedelic substances to them in order to give the animals advice—simultaneously shifting to a kind of trans-species pidgin with surprising properties.”

Pidgin is a language created between two languages (Italian and Greek; Grecanic).

“If, Juaniku said, a jaguar sees you as a being capable of looking it in the eye—a self like itself, a 'you'—it will leave you alone. But if it should see you as prey—an 'it'—you could become dead meat. The way other kinds of beings see us matters. The fact that other beings can see us changes everything. If jaguars also have a representation of us—and if the way they do so is of vital importance to us—then anthropology cannot simply limit itself to exploring the representations that different societies produce in this regard.”
