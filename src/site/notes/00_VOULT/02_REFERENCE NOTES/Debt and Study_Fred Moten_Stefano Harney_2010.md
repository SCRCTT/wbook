Debt and Credit

They say we have too much debt. We need better credit, more credit, less spending.

Credit is a means of privatization and debt a means of socialization.

Debt is social and credit is asocial. Debt is mutual. Credit runs only one way. Debt runs in every direction, scattering, escaping, seeking refuge. The debtor seeks refuge among other debtors, acquires debt from them, offers debt to them.

This refuge, this place of bad debt, is what we would call the fugitive public. Running through the public and the private, the state and the economy, the fugitive public can be identified by its bad debt—but only by its debtors.

To creditors, it is just a place where something is wrong, though that something—the invaluable thing that has no value—is desired.

Credit keeps track. Debt forgets.

The student is a bad debtor threatened with credit.

The student has a bad habit. She studies. She studies but she does not learn. If she learned, they could measure her progress, confirm her attributes, give her credit.

The debt cannot be forgiven; it can only be forgotten and remembered.

Creditors forgive debt by offering credit, by offering more from the very source of the pain of debt, a pain for which there is only one source of justice: bad debt, forgetting, remembering again, remembering it cannot be paid, cannot be credited, stamped "received".

Some personal thoughts about the article

I know that this type of article touches on aspects related to cultures, black studies, and power dynamics. However, the open language manages to show a photographic complexity of this dynamic today in other contexts as well. At a certain point in the article, Bifo is cited. From there, I thought of a speech he gave in Rome many years ago, and so I begin to recount it:

"During a conference in Osaka, Japan, on media activism movements. After a mapping of international contributions in this regard: therefore to French post-structuralist rhizomatic thought, to Italian movements of autonomy and post-workerist thought, to the British contribution of punk culture." (we think of Mark Fisher)

Bifo came to wonder what kind of contribution Japan is proposing in the experience of activism that passes through the media.

For this reason, Bifo believes that Western movements are already a past. (The conference was held in the early 2000s, so for us it has become an even deeper past). So what does Japan bring to the table in this perspective? The figure of the Hikikomori.

The pressure placed by debt and credit is so high that one finds themselves in the condition of the student at the end of the article. A student who has no passion for what she has found, and is surprised if she is heard. She is seen as conspiratorial, heretical, criminal, amateur. Therefore, incapable of being in debt and she will never have credit. From the article, it says: "We will not handle credit, and we cannot handle debt, debt flows through us, and there's no time to tell you everything...".

The hikikomori is the one who locks himself in his room. The one who refuses to leave it for any reason. He is fed by his parents through a small slot in the door of the room, in the most extreme cases, or by friends who come to visit him weekly. He lives in perpetual connection with the virtual world. The authorities are beginning to worry about the fact that the officially registered hikikomori are about one million. In fact, we must think that there are many more (Here is how the authorities worry and see the student as a sort of conspiracy, mysterious, inexplicable, and therefore frightening). It is a trend. For Bifo, this is a trend toward the search for autonomy in the present world. Withdrawing.

As the student finds herself outside the discourse of debt-credit, she finds herself outside those dynamics, not by will, perhaps by necessity like the hikikomori.

Bifo writes:

Hikikomori is the avant garde because he is doing the only one healthy gesture in a world of competition, exploitation, aggressivity and social humiliation: refusing socialisation, breaking the tie, escaping in the space of virtuality and darkness and loneliness. It is not funny, I know. I do not want to be an hikikomori, and I do not suggest to anybody to be an hikikomori. But I recognize the meaning, and the implicit intention. It's better to be self-recluded than living all the day in the terror, anxiety, aggression and humiliation. It's better to live with no money than giving away your life for a salary.

More than ten years have passed since this reflection by Bifo and things change very quickly. I wonder if this type of "escape" could also be connected to the recent definition of "Brainrot," that is, the alleged deterioration of a person's mental or intellectual state, especially as a consequence of excessive consumption of material (particularly online content) considered trivial or unchallenging.

In this case, the escape takes the form of annihilation and dopamine.

The precarity found in the midst of this power dynamic, this pincer between debt and credit, distracts us from planning. What I wonder is: from these types of escapes, what can we interpret? How can we listen to the whisper of the student who says "Come, let's plan something together"?
