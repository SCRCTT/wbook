#article #conference 
For eight years, in July, I have been writing a birthday message for the Rekombinant list, which began its meager existence in July 2000.

I see no reason why I should not continue the tradition. July this year is, for me, the month of the hikikomori.

In Japan, I was overwhelmed by the knowledge of this phenomenon, which I had heard of previously (il manifesto had talked about it some time ago). The meaning and the potential of the hikikomori experience is only now beginning to manifest for me, after having met Kaz, Shunya, Jun, Yaoy, Futoshi, and above all Naohisa Oda, a boy with whom I discussed at length after the Osaka conference.

The conference was titled Art and Activism, and it was conceived as a reconstruction of the media-activism experience from the European point of view.

At the beginning, a film by Spegni la tivu about telestreets is presented (very beautiful, I hadn't seen it yet).

I tried to quickly synthesize some notes on the history of media-activism, starting from the seventies, when transmission technologies began to spread socially, up to the nineties of cyberculture and net-activism.

I outlined the great international contributions to the formation of a media-active movement. From the French philosophical contribution, in the forms of situ thought and the forms of post-structuralist rhizomatic thought. To the Italian contribution in the forms of the movements of autonomy and post-workerist thought. To the British contribution of punk culture, to the Californian contribution of psychedelia and visionary cyberculture. To the Latin American contribution, in the forms of the re-emergence of an indigenous culture that carries a proliferation of temporalities.

But now I am in Japan and I ask myself what is the contribution that Japanese culture brings to the global media-active experience today.

The interest of Japanese artists and activists in autonomous Western cultures is a sign of cosmopolitan cultural vitality, but there is a risk of reproducing the stereotype of Japanese imitation of what is done in the West. I proposed to overturn the perspective.

The future is not in Western movements, which are indeed the past. The future lies rather in the figure of the hikikomori.

The new Japanese generation carries written in its brain and on its skin the signs of the techno-epistemic performation of the smoothest capitalism on the planet.

The question of smoothness is addressed by Deleuze and Guattari in A Thousand Plateaus.

Glabrous culture tends to take the place of the hairy cultures of the past era, when connection takes the place of conjunction.

Connection presupposes bodies purified of the hairiness of memory, of the desiring singularity. This is the operation that imperial conformism and global corporate capitalism have performed on the body and mind of the new Japanese generation.

Nothing more stands in the way of the perfect flow of information, the universal valorizing fluid. Competition between human beings takes place, in this country, under conditions of particular harshness. The Japanese body, so apparently fragile and tender, has been submitted to a machinic concatenation purified of every element of individual freedom.

Work activity occupies the entirety of life time. Life is entirely occupied by economic abstraction, which becomes abstraction of meaning and of life. The hikikomori is the one who locks himself in his room. The one who refuses to leave it for any reason. He is fed by his parents through a small door in the room's door, in the most extreme cases, or by friends who come to visit him weekly. He lives in perpetual connection with the virtual world. The authorities are beginning to worry about the fact that the officially registered hikikomori are about one million. In fact, we must think that there are many more, since we must consider the mass of those who perhaps leave their little room a couple of times a week, or even those who, while continuing to live in society, are preparing to leave it definitively. It is a trend. And in my opinion, it is the main trend of the search for autonomy in the present world. To withdraw, to interrupt the supply of energy towards this social.

Interpreting the meaning of this kind of behavior, and expressing the trend that this subtraction represents, is a fundamental task, on the theoretical level and on the level of a politics of the time to come. A couple of days ago I received a message from Naohisa Oda, which I publish here in Rekombinant, in his barbaric English (just as my English is barbaric, in the following message, with which I reply to him).

The exchange with Naohisa allows for an insight into the new problems and the new tensions that animate (or disanimate) the new generations of the mutant sun.

MY REPLY My dear Naohisa Oda,

I thank you for your message. It's very important for me, because I can understand much more from your message than reading hundred pages of statistics about the young culture in Japan.

Your questions are answers.

But now I will try to explain my point of view. During the Osaka conference I told:

"Japan is the future, the hikikomori is the avant garde, and we have to learn from his experience."

I do not mean that the current Japanese culture is good, I do not say that hikikomori is happy.. All the contrary. I say that hikikomori is the trend, the inmost tendency of next culture. And we have to understand it, to interpret it, if we want to act positively in society. As you say Japanese seem to be ants, or bees.

Actually I think that the destiny of the whole mankind in this age, is being more and more dominated by a network of linguistic machines, shaping perception, imagination, cognition and memory: becoming ants, in the sense of automated executors.

Young Japanese seem to be the avant garde of this process because they are (as you say) culturally predisposed to social automation, to behaviour conformation.

But this process of cablation of human sociability, this technical subjugation of the mind and of the body does not happen without suffering. Suffering is the key-word of the new generation, that you may call connective generation.

A generation of humans who have learned more words by an electronic machine than by the mother.

Suicide is the most political act of the last decade.

The most important event of the new century is the suicide of 19 young muslims driving two airplanes against the towers.

Suicide seems to be an artistic gesture, in people like Cho Hseng hui, the Korean who killed 32 students in west virginia, while killing himself.

And that Finnish guy who killed eight people and himslef with a teeshirt saying: humanity is overrated. Hikikomori is the avant garde because he is doing the only one healthy gesture in a world of competition, exploitation, aggressivity and social humiliation: refusing socialisation, breaking the tie, escaping in the space of virtuality and darkness and loneliness.

It is not funny, I know. I do not want to be an hikikomori, and I do not suggest to anybody to be an hikikomori. But I recognize the meaning, and the implicit intention. It's better to be self-recluded than living all the day in the terror, anxiety, aggression and humiliation. It's better to live with no money than giving away your life for a salary. In the '70's autonomy was the movement of those people who refused the exploitation and competition and the stress of capitalist rule. They could do it in a collective form, like the hippies of California in the '60's. They created colonies of people getting away from the kingdom of slavery, toward the republic of freedom and refusal of work.

Then the neoliberal counter-revolution destroyed every space of commonality and privatized every form of life and forced every person to run and run and run for a gloomy survival.

Today it seems impossible to make things in a collective way, unless the collective is imposed by the corporation.

So hikikomori finds his way in isolation. You ask me if in Italy there are hikikomori. Of course yes. May be it is more difficult to stay alone all the time, because the Italian cities are different, and urban isolation is more difficult.

But hikikomori exist, although they are not yet marked as a social problem. My friend Marco is an hikikomori, because of his behaviour of self-reclusion, and because of the psycho-imaginary elaboration that he is developing.

I think that this trend is going to spread everywhere in the planet.

Until it will become such a massive wave of withdrawal that the energies will be pulled away from the capitalist system, and the final collapse will come. Who knows? Well, I know that I have not answered all your questions, but I hope we will go on talking.

Now I go to have a rest.

I'm so tired.

bifo
