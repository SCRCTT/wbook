[[00_VOULT/02_REFERENCE NOTES/Munari method\|Munari method]]

Bruno Munari (1907–1998) was a Italian artist, designer, and writer who redefined the relationship between art and industry. Alongside figures like Lucio Fontana, he was a key protagonist of the post-war economic boom, acting as a "visual operator" and consultant who bridged the gap between creative expression and industrial production.

From his early departure from Futurism marked by his "aerial machine" (1930) and his famous "useless machines" (1933) Munari championed a sense of lightness and humor. In 1948, he co-founded the **MAC (Movement for Concrete Art)** to advocate for a synthesis of the arts, demonstrating how art and technology could converge.

A pioneer of multi-sensory and environmental art, he created _Concavo-convesso_ (1947), one of the first installations in art history. His experimentation with light—through projected painting and polarized filters exhibited at **MoMA** in 1954 established him as a leader in programmed and kinetic art. Ultimately, Munari remains impossible to categorize, escaping definition through his restless creativity and refined, multifaceted practice.
