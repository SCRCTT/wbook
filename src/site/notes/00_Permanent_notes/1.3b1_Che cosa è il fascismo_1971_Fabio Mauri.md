## What is Fascism (Che cosa è il fascismo)

The historical performance _What is Fascism_ was presented on April 2, 1971, at the Safa Palatino Film Studios in Rome. The event took place with the participation of students from the "Silvio D’Amico" National Academy of Dramatic Art, concluding the seminar "Gesture and Behavior in Art Today" curated by Giorgio Pressburger.

The inspiration was the memory of Hitler's visit to Florence in 1938, an occasion where the Bologna team—which included Fabio Mauri alongside his friend Pier Paolo Pasolini, Fabio Luca Cavazza, and others—won the youth intellectual competition. The action consists of a reconstruction of the ‘Ludi Juveniles’ (Youth Games). Gymnastic drills, fencing matches, skating exhibitions, flag-waving, hymns, and individual debates on the "Regime Mystique" follow one after another.

In the performance, the entire action unfolds on a rectangular carpet with a swastika symbol placed at the center of the red rug, set between black grandstands subdivided by "Corporations" (Artists, Agriculturalists, Builders, Engineers, etc.), where a corresponding audience is seated. Two small stands bear the Star of David, signifying the racial discrimination of the Fascist era—presented in Italy at the time as something harmless and routine. The invitations to the exhibition, in various colors depending on social category, were also reserved for Jewish men and women, who were discriminated against by being restricted to these small stands.

Music from the classical Fascist repertoire precedes and accompanies the proceedings—both real and imaginary—simply evoked from the Command Podium, from which the great assembly of the _Gioventù Italiana del Littorio_ (Italian Youth of the Lictor) is directively orchestrated. At the end of the competitions, "Film Luce" newsreels are projected onto a vintage screen, pervaded by the evident falsehood of propaganda.

In _What is Fascism_, the contrast between the apparent normality of the events and the presence of negative signals generates a sense of progressive unease and reveals the mortal impudence of the State Lie, as well as the unfounded optimism of a people—or rather, of two. (**Dora Aceto**)

## Technical (Yet Disorganized) Notes on the Action "What is Fascism", 1971

_(Mimeographed sheet distributed on the evening of April 2, 1971, at the Safa Palatino Plants, Rome)_

"What is Fascism" is a complex action. I believe it distinguishes itself from the "gesture" of the movement in painting—referred to by that name—due to its deliberately programmed component. Or, if it is equal to the "gesture" even in the prefiguration of results, my type of active composition directly welcomes certain rhythms of theatricality. It includes them in an articulated whole whose final form remains unknown. To approach a complex, highly expressive image using people and things as simple materials, trusting in the concrete meaning of a mental obsession. To release this image into a real form, even if only for the duration of an hour.

Whoever wears the chosen uniform enters a field of relationships; they become the spectacle. Whoever repeats a text in their own way: ditto. Whoever witnesses the gesture and fills a role assigned to them by the "Grandstands" in which they are placed becomes the spectacle, without the need for a subsequent will to participate.

There is no _mise-en-scène_ (direction) in the sense of a closed prefiguration of an ideal form. In "What is Fascism," we do not aspire to an ideal form. Chance, and its imperfections, provide the ideological plot with the concreteness of the human and the current.

A type of experimental theater initiated in the 1700s can provide a practical indication of this event. I am referring to "spiritual exercises." To nurture a horror of hell, one concentrates on its evils, physically experiencing for a moment the burn of fire, etc. Here, one experiences in a short time the false ideology: the abyss of institutionalized Superficiality, the Tautology of absolute Power, the intimate malice of the Lie hidden within Order, the shame of cultural confusion, the irresponsibility of those who claim for themselves the freedom of collective judgment, and the deception of youth that brings grace and trust to serve as a prelude to its own slaughter.

Error binds with anything else, especially with truth and beauty. The foolishness of innocent nature is the naive accomplice of every evil. The seductive nothingness of when—appearing to finally resolve the complexity of reality into a simple fact—the void finds space and takes shape in minds and bodies, mimicking the serious, the true, and the deep. In "What is Fascism," there flows a note of nostalgia for youth, and of resentment for having had to live it within a mythical, empty, and blind scene.

Technically, "What is Fascism" lacks direction, but it possesses a backup, simultaneous one. From the **Command Podium** (from which the militias of the G.I.L.* are verbally deployed), the action can be somewhat conducted according to sudden needs. The actors know that their role (whether as followers or hierarchs) is superior to their "part." This is the _fictio_ that engages them in their methodology as actors. No "estrangement" (which implies continuity and a certain fixity of relationships) is possible. The bet is to want to be, for one hour, the thing they seem to be, individually.

"What is Fascism" is a didactic dream where conflicting distances—Jews and Fascists, for example—are placed peacefully side by side with the apparent normality of a dream, ready to turn into a nightmare, intimately inclined toward a symbolic meaning more exact and complex than their simplified apparent docility. The dramatic nature of this "ideology-based action" is therefore individual, even for the spectator. Socially, it can be perceived in the worst way: as simply polite.

A thread of irony, obtained through elementary juxtapositions, runs throughout the program. It is one of the "negative signs" that, within the spectacle, communicate the critical instance to the viewer. Other "negative signs" include "The End" on the white screen where the LUCE Documentary is projected; the labels on the grandstands; and the German general, in whose honor the celebration is held, who is already and forever "made of wax." These are signals that cannot be mistaken in their meaning, indispensable for anchoring the critical point of view from which to look at the aberrant elements exhibited in such a serene form.

My personal experience as a playwright is certainly used here somewhere, but it is secondary. "What is Fascism" is, in me, first and foremost an image—a physical, sonic form where a certain number of opposing meanings balance the image itself with its critical meaning as a backbone. Something akin to a poem.

_*Rome, April 2, 1971. "Memory Book" edited by Carolyn Christov Bakargiev and Janet Cardiff. Thames and Hudson Publishers. G.I.L.: Gioventù Italiana del Littorio. Fascist youth organization._
